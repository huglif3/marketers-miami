EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

What happened

MARKETERS IN THE ARENA CDMX brought together a smaller but higher-quality room than raw registration volume suggested. The strongest signal was not scale, but density: founders, operators, institutional fintech teams, and ecosystem builders who were materially relevant to Stellar, stablecoins, and GTM in LATAM.

Timing mattered. Running the event the day after Stablecoin Conference helped capture people already in Mexico City, while Espacio Cripto content capture extended the event into a post-event distribution channel through interviews, clips, and organic social posts.

The room still exposed clear next-iteration needs: bilingual workshop design, stronger execution-layer content, tighter attendee filtering, and better speaker access before and after talks.

WHAT WORKED

Positive signal

Room Quality

The room skewed founder-heavy and operator-centric, with strong representation from LATAM ecosystem builders and institutions including Grupo Salinas, BANORTE, and Mercado Pago.

Strategic Timing

Holding CDMX immediately after Stablecoin Conference captured attendees who were already in-market and ready for a more curated operator room.

Content Capture

Espacio Cripto interviews and speaker clips created a post-event content runway instead of leaving the event value trapped in the room.

Organic Social Proof

The #EnLaArena hashtag picked up organically across attendee posts, with real-time tags to @StellarOrg and @myosin_xyz that extended the event’s visibility beyond the room.

Satisfaction

Among post-event responses, 13 of 18 attendees gave the event a 5/5 rating, representing roughly 72% top-box satisfaction among respondents.

Partner Discovery

Partner networks including Espacio Media, BAF, and Universidad de la Libertad helped drive high-intent discovery rather than generic attendance inflation.

WHAT TO IMPROVE

Operational learnings

Content Format

The agenda leaned too heavily on keynotes and firesides. Attendees responded best to execution-layer signal and wanted more hands-on workshop output, not more top-down talking.

Workshop Language Fit

Workshops were delivered in Spanish only, which reduced value for English-speaking attendees. Future editions should language-match workshops to local audience mix.

Speaker Access

Attendees wanted speaker contacts and social handles shared during or immediately after sessions. That should be built into slides, event pages, and printed materials.

Technical Production

There was a technical issue during Wendy Lopez’s presentation. Future events should include mandatory AV checks for every speaker before doors open.

Show Rate

Approved registrations outpaced attendance materially. Recommendation: tighten approvals, send a 48-hour confirmation nudge, and consider a more active waitlist flow.

Venue Fit

Some feedback questioned whether Universidad de la Libertad was the best location given the distance, though many still pointed to its production quality as a strong upside. This is worth revisiting as the series sharpens its physical-world identity.

FORMAT CHANGES

Use for next editions

Increase Hands-on Workshops

Set a minimum of two execution-layer workshops per event, each with a clear outcome or takeaway attendees can use immediately.

Share Speaker Contacts

Publish speaker handles and contact pathways during sessions and on event pages instead of relying on informal post-event requests.

Language Match

Workshop content should match the language profile of the room or provide bilingual support where needed.

Mandatory AV Check

Every presenter should complete a pre-event technical check. This should become standard operating procedure, not a nice-to-have.

Tighter Approval Flow

Tighten the approval list, add a 48-hour confirmation nudge, and manage a real waitlist to improve show rate.

Protect the Editorial Feel

Future venue choices should reinforce the event’s editorial identity as strongly as the speaker lineup and partner mix do.
